Vigenère Cipher Definition and Example

The Vigenère Cipher is a polyalphabetic substitution cipher that uses a keyword to encrypt and decrypt text. It was invented by Blaise de Vigenère in the 16th century and offers a more secure method of encryption compared to simple substitution ciphers.

How Vigenère Cipher Works:

  1. Key: The Vigenère Cipher uses a keyword as its encryption key. The keyword is repeated to match the length of the plaintext.
  2. Encryption: To encrypt a message, each letter of the plaintext is shifted according to the corresponding letter in the keyword. For example, if the keyword is "KEY," and the plaintext letter is 'A,' it is shifted by 'K,' 'E,' and 'Y' successively to produce the ciphertext letter.
  3. Decryption: Decryption involves reversing the process. Each letter of the ciphertext is shifted backward according to the corresponding letter in the keyword to obtain the original plaintext letter.

Example:

Let's illustrate the Vigenère Cipher with an example. We'll use the keyword "KEY" and encrypt the plaintext "HELLO."

Keyword: KEY

Plaintext: HELLO

Now, we align the keyword with the plaintext:

        Keyword:    K   E   Y   K   E
        Plaintext:  H   E   L   L   O

We shift each letter of the plaintext according to the corresponding letter in the keyword:

        Ciphertext: R   I   X   Q   B

So, the encrypted message "HELLO" with the keyword "KEY" becomes "RIXQB" using the Vigenère Cipher.

The Vigenère Cipher is more secure than simple substitution ciphers because it introduces variability in the encryption process through the use of keywords. However, longer keywords provide better security.
