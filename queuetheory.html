Queue Data Structure

A queue is a linear data structure that follows the First In, First Out (FIFO) principle. In a queue, elements are added to the rear (end) and removed from the front. It's commonly used for various applications, including task scheduling, print job management, and more.

A queue is a fundamental data structure that follows the First In, First Out (FIFO) principle. It behaves much like a real-world queue of people waiting in line, where the person who arrives first is the first to be served.

Queues are used in a wide range of applications, including:

  • Task scheduling in operating systems.
  • Print job management in printers.
  • Breadth-first search algorithms in graph theory.
  • Request handling in web servers.

Working of a Queue

Imagine a queue as a line of items where you can only interact with the front and rear. There are two primary operations associated with a queue:

  • Enqueue: This operation adds an item to the rear of the queue. It's analogous to a person joining the line.
  • Dequeue: This operation removes an item from the front of the queue. It's like serving the person at the front of the line.

Let's illustrate the working of a queue with a simple example:

  1. Initially, the queue is empty.
  2. We perform an Enqueue operation to add "Item 1" to the rear of the queue. The queue now looks like this:
    3. Item 1
  3. We perform another Enqueue operation to add "Item 2" to the rear of the queue. The queue now looks like this:
    4. Item 1
    Item 2
  4. Next, we perform a Dequeue operation to remove "Item 1" from the front of the queue. The queue becomes:
    5. Item 2
  5. We perform another Enqueue operation to add "Item 3" to the rear of the queue. The queue now looks like this:
    6. Item 2
    Item 3

This process continues, and items are added to the rear and removed from the front of the queue, following the FIFO principle.
